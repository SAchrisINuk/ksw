Part one · Why now

Our pupils will work alongside AI for their whole careers

The OECD has spent a decade studying which skills hold their value as AI spreads. Judgement, communication and digital fluency rise. Routine knowledge falls. Its Learning Compass names the three that matter most: creating new value, reconciling dilemmas, and taking responsibility.

The same research carries a warning. Pupils who hand their thinking to AI produce better work in the moment and learn less from it. The gains vanish in the exam hall. So the task is to teach AI use, deliberately, from the right age, on tools the school governs. That's a teaching challenge first and a technology decision second, and this strategy treats it that way.

10 yrs

A pupil joining King's Hawford this September sits GCSEs in the mid-2030s. Every decision here is tested against what that pupil will need.

Part two · The choice

Google or Microsoft: the honest comparison

Firefly retires by Easter 2027, so the Foundation must choose its platform this year. Two credible options exist: stay Microsoft and build on Teams and Copilot, or move to Google Workspace with Classroom and Gemini. The principle set by our own operations team decides it: teaching needs drive IT provision.

Microsoft's academic price is $18 per user per month, roughly £14. Across a Foundation of our size that's six figures a year to match what Gemini includes for nothing, and it still leaves every pupil under 13 without provision. Microsoft's free Copilot Chat (13+, admin-enabled) is a browser chat without the in-app integration.

Where Microsoft is genuinely ahead

Honesty strengthens the case. We're a Microsoft school today: staff know Outlook and Word, some departments run OneNote well, and staying put would cost nothing in transition effort. Excel remains the stronger spreadsheet for advanced work, and Teams is what most pupils will meet in employment. None of that is dismissed. The judgement is that a one-off, well-supported migration costs less than paying every year, and every pupil, for an AI provision that excludes half the Foundation by age.

The recommendation

Google Workspace for Education Plus across all three schools, Google Classroom as the single VLE replacing Firefly, and Gemini for Education as the Foundation's governed AI. Devices are deliberately excluded and follow as a separate costed workstream. Moving the Senior School moves the direction for the preps too, so this is asked for as one conscious Foundation-level decision.

Part four · The rules

Responsible AI, in plain English

The working draft of the Foundation's responsible-AI policy. Written to be read, not filed. Each section expands.

  • Safety first. The DfE's guidance is clear that safety leads every decision about generative AI in schools. Where safety and convenience conflict, safety wins.
  • Human in the loop. AI never makes a judgement about a pupil on its own. A teacher checks, decides and remains accountable. AI is never the sole marker of pupil work.
  • Taught, not found. Pupils meet AI through supervised, age-appropriate teaching on school-governed tools, not through the app store.
  • Thinking stays with the pupil. AI use that replaces a pupil's own reasoning is misuse, whatever tool it happens on.
  • Transparent with families. Parents can always find out which tools their child uses, what data those tools hold and who to ask.
  • Only tools on the Foundation's approved list are used for school work. Approval needs a risk assessment covering safeguarding, data protection, age terms and educational value.
  • Gemini for Education, once enabled, is the approved AI platform: education terms, no human review of conversations, no training on our data, admin-controlled.
  • No personal data about any pupil or member of staff goes into an unapproved AI tool. No free consumer chatbot meets our data standard.
  • The list is public to staff, reviewed termly, and anything can be proposed for it.
  • Staff are encouraged to use approved AI to cut workload: adapting resources, drafting non-sensitive materials, generating practice questions.
  • Every AI output used with pupils is checked by the teacher for accuracy, bias, reading age and curriculum fit first.
  • AI may assist report drafting only inside the approved environment and never as the final voice. The teacher's knowledge of the child is the report.
  • Training follows the DfE's published AI modules, in directed time, with no assumption of prior confidence.
  • King's Hawford and King's St Alban's. Pupils learn about AI before they use it. Direct use of generative tools is by exception, teacher-led, on the screen at the front.
  • Senior School, Years 7–11. Structured, supervised use in taught contexts. Prompting, verification and referencing are taught as explicit skills.
  • Sixth Form. The widest access and the highest expectations: approved AI as a study tool under §5, and leadership of the Foundation's AI Society.

For any work contributing to a qualification, JCQ's AI Use in Assessments guidance (revision 2, April 2025) applies without exception. Every department publishes how it applies in their subject.

  • Work submitted must be the pupil's own. AI content passed off as the pupil's own is malpractice.
  • Where AI is permitted in research stages it must be referenced: tool named, date recorded, and a non-editable copy of prompt and output (a screenshot) kept and submitted with the work.
  • Teachers authenticate work using their knowledge of the pupil. Where a teacher can't sign the authentication statement, the malpractice procedure applies.
  • The exams officer and heads of department review these procedures each September against current JCQ publications.

Keeping Children Safe in Education 2026, in force from this September, treats AI-enabled harm as mainstream safeguarding. Our policy follows it.

  • AI-generated intimate images and deepfakes are child-on-child abuse under KCSIE 2026, handled through safeguarding procedures, never as a technology incident.
  • AI chat tools that simulate people or relationships are a contact risk. Companion-style chatbots are not approved for pupils at any age.
  • Filtering and monitoring cover AI tools, with the annual effectiveness review KCSIE now requires reported to governors.
  • All staff read KCSIE Part 1 in full, and training includes recognising AI-related harm: coercion, impersonation, image-based abuse.
  • Concerns go to the Designated Safeguarding Lead by the normal route. AI changes the medium, not the duty.
  • Reviewed each September against the current DfE policy paper, JCQ publications and KCSIE, and each January by the Foundation Digital Strategy Group.
  • Owned by the Deputy Head (Academic and Innovation); operated by the AI and Digital Learning Lead; safeguarding sections owned jointly with the DSL.
  • Version history is published at the foot of this document. Staff, pupils and parents can propose changes at any time.

  • Annually: filtering and monitoring review, and policy re-approval.
